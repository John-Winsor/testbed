Arthur Thomas and the Great War
by John Winsor, April 2009.

HOME


Arthur Thomas Handley was born in June 1895 in West Ham, East London.  Subsequently the family moved to Ilford, Essex where his father Arthur Handley opened a fish and poultry shop at 72 - 74 High Road, Ilford.  The family lived above the shop.  At the time the Great War broke out in 1914 Arthur Thomas was indentured as an apprentice to an engineering company in East London.

I was told by my grandmother that Arthur had been deeply upset because he was given a "white feather" by some young women at work.  I believe this symbolic act of cruelty was not uncommon in the social climate of the time.  Whether or not that was the motive, Arthur resolved to join the army.  But he was bound by his Articles of Apprenticeship to remain at work.  So in March 1915, aged nearly 20, he decided the issue by running away and signing-up with the army.

On the next page is displayed Arthur's first letter home.  He is obviously concerned about the political repercussions of his actions.

There was a story that Arthur was first sent to a training camp in north Essex after which he was put on a train to Portsmouth for embarkation.  When the train was approaching Ilford station Arthur wrote his father a hasty note and tied it to a penknife.  As the train shot through the station he threw it out of the window.  The penknife with note attached was retrieved from the platform and delivered to his family.  In consequence his parents journeyed to Portsmouth and were briefly re-united with Arthur before he embarked.

Arthur served with the Royal Fusiliers on the Western Front in France and Belgium.  In July 1916 he survived the Battle of the Somme along with only 11 others from his Company after being ordered to go "over the top".  It is a matter of sad record that Arthur was killed in August 1917 by a German shell.  This happened due to sporadic shelling and was not part of any specific battle.  He was then aged 22 years.  It is surprising, perhaps, that he survived as long as he did.

Arthur's grave is in Hooge Crater Military Cemetery, Belgium.  This is on route N8 about 2½ miles east of Ypres town centre.  For those who would visit, Arthur's grave is first in row 6H.

In consequence of the Great War there was terrible grief throughout the land.  This encouraged the cult of spiritualism as distraught parents sought to contact their dead sons.  In this sad climate my grandmother attended a spiritualist meeting where she heard words spoken that caused her fear and distress.  She walked out of the meeting and never returned.  I think the Handley family were broken by Arthur's death and I am not sure they ever fully recovered.


When you are standing at your hero's grave,
Or near some homeless village where he died,
Remember, through your heart's rekindling pride,
The German soldiers who were loyal and brave.

Men fought like brutes; and hideous things were done;
And you have nourished hatred harsh and blind.
But in that Golgotha perhaps you'll find
The mothers of the men who killed your son.

(Golgotha means Calvary)                 Siegfried Sassoon.


Musical note:
The music accompanying this section is the moving "Hymn to the Fallen" composed by John Williams and featured in the film "Saving Private Ryan".
